## Definition in ICD-11

- Burn-out is classified in ICD‑11 under **“Factors influencing health status or contact with health services”**, not as a medical disease.[web:118]
- It is defined as a **“syndrome conceptualized as resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed.”**[web:118]
- The syndrome has three dimensions:
  - Feelings of energy depletion or exhaustion.
  - Increased mental distance from one’s job, or feelings of negativism or cynicism related to one's job.
  - Reduced professional efficacy.[web:118]
- Burn-out **“refers specifically to phenomena in the occupational context and should not be applied to describe experiences in other areas of life.”**[web:118]

## Evolution vs ICD-10

- Burn-out was already mentioned in ICD‑10 in the same category, but **the ICD‑11 definition is more detailed and operational**.[web:118]
- This gives stronger legitimacy to using burn-out as a structured concept in prevention and assessment tools (like V.I.T.A.L).

## WHO Future Guidelines

- WHO states it is **“about to embark on the development of evidence-based guidelines on mental well-being in the workplace.”**[web:118]
- This means workplace mental health is considered a strategic public health topic at WHO level, with formal guidance forthcoming.

## Relevance for V.I.T.A.L

- ICD‑11 recognition makes burn-out **a serious, internationally standardized occupational phenomenon**, not just a buzzword.
- V.I.T.A.L can safely rely on the ICD‑11 three-dimension framework (exhaustion, mental distance, reduced efficacy) as a backbone for:
  - Symptom questions during weekly check-ups.
  - Risk scoring combining subjective answers and biometric data.
- Positioning: V.I.T.A.L is an **early-detection and prevention tool** for an ICD‑11-recognized phenomenon, not a diagnostic or treatment tool.
